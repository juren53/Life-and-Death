


It came again when I was in my thirties and did my flight training out of Burbank Airport. I soloed off the same runway, out of the same airport Jack Hennessy had departed from on his last flight. From time to time as I was doing my pre-flights, or waiting in line on a taxi way or flying over the old Goodland neighborhood I would think about the sacrafice the Jack Hennessy made to save lives .  I tried to image what a pilot sees in those final seconds when the instruments have told you what they have to tell you and the only remaining question is what kind of man you will be in the time that is left.

Over the years, without me consciously thinking about it, the memory of the events in September 1966 would suddenly jump back front and center.  They would periodically came back to me twenty years later when I did my flight training out of Burbank, soloing off the same runway, out of the same airport Jack Hennessy had departed from on his last flight. I tried not to think about it but because I wanted to stay focused and then I could hear a seasoned voice in the back of my head - - it could have been my flight instructor, it could have been Jack Hennessy -- say, relax, stay focused, pay attention to detail, don't hurry, be patient, make sure you go through every step in the check lists top-to-bottom, don't take short cuts.  As I was doing my pre-flights, or waiting in line on a taxiway or flying over the old Goodland neighborhood, it was as though Jack Hennessy was saying to me everything will be ok if you follow and the procedures.
