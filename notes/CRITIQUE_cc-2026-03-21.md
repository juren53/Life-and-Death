# Editorial Critique: Life-and-Death.md
*Claude Code review — 2026-03-21*

---

## Overall Assessment

A genuinely moving piece with a strong, measured voice. The Dodger framing earns its place and the title pays off. The essay has a clear emotional architecture that works.

---

## Structural / Paragraph-level

- **Hennessy biography paragraph (para. 9)** is the longest in the essay and carries the most emotional weight — but tries to do too much in one block. The transition from his war record to his final act to his burial deserves more breathing room. Consider splitting at *"He survived all of it."*

- **Research/internet paragraph (para. 15)** is the weakest in the piece. The list — *"Message groups, user forums, online databases, digital archives, social networks and electronic messaging technologies that we generally lump under the Internet umbrella"* — reads as clinical and already dated. The technology is beside the point; the *need* to find answers is the point. Could be cut to two sentences.

- **Paragraph 18** (the unasked questions about Jim Bissell) is very long and starts to circle. The rhetorical questions — *"What it was like to fly with him. What kind of man he was..."* — are strong, but the paragraph could end sooner and hit harder.

---

## Sentence-level

| Location | Issue | Suggestion |
|---|---|---|
| Para. 9, final sentence | *"The maneuver cost him his last available options, and sadly, his life."* — "sadly" drastically undersells the emotional climax | Cut "sadly" entirely; the sentence is stronger without it |
| Para. 26 (final) | *"how close the distance was between ordinary and gone"* — mixed metaphor | "how thin the line was between ordinary and gone" |
| Para. 25 | *"a moment where most are left with nothing but panic"* | "where" → "when" (temporal context) |
| Para. 23 | *"Things had changed. Something had shifted."* — redundant | Cut one; "Something had shifted" is the stronger of the two |
| Para. 23 | *"the fever pitch of my obsession was gone"* — cliché | Find a fresher phrase |
| Para. 3 | *"But then there are times when more important things grab a thirteen-year-old's attention like an important baseball game"* | Tense shift (present in past-tense narrative); "important" used twice |
| Para. 4 | *"low, deep throated, menacing growl"* | Should be "deep-throated" (hyphenated compound adjective) |

---

## Smaller Observations

- *"ad infinitum"* (para. 1) — slightly formal for the conversational tone established everywhere else.
- *"a pilot's worst nightmare"* — cliché worth replacing, especially since the author is a pilot writing about a pilot.
- **Proposed epitaph** — *"Here lies a hero who in his last act saved countless lives as he lost his"* is moving but syntactically tangled. Cleaner alternative: *"Here lies a man who saved countless lives as he lost his own."*
- *"wing-tip to wing-tip"* — standard aviation usage is "wingtip to wingtip" (no hyphens).

---

## What's Working Exceptionally Well

- **The sound description of the approaching plane** — *"not the steady, predictable rise in volume of a plane on a normal approach path, but something asymmetric and urgent"* — is masterful.
- **The dinner table scene** with the mother cutting off the conversation — economical and completely emotionally true. One of the best passages in the piece.
- **The acoustic metaphor** closing para. 23 — *"the way a door closing in a far room suddenly changes the acoustics of the whole house"* — is excellent.
- **"He had, in effect, engineered the crash. Controlled the uncontrollable."** — that's the whole essay in two sentences.
- The Dodger obsession as framing device works perfectly with the title and earns its return at the end.
